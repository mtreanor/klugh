This file will outline the history of klugh.


- For me, this starts in 2010 with the creation of Comme il Faut (along with [Josh McCoy]("https://joshmccoy.com"), [Ben Samuel]("http://www.ben-samuel.com"), [Aaron Reed]("https://aaronareed.net") and others). We were all pursuing PhDs as part of the [Expressive Intelligence Studio]("https://eis.ucsc.edu") at University of California at Santa Cruz and being advised by [Michael Mateas]("https://users.soe.ucsc.edu/~michaelm/") and [Noah Wardrip-Fruin]("http://www.noahwf.com")
- This project has a rich history that goes back before my time with GOFAI (good old fashioned AI). I was very inspired by my advisors, and am extremey lucky that I was able to learn so much from them.
- For that project, the main idea was to create social norms that applied to everyone in a simulation, and that would create a kind of "social physics". 
- Social physics is metaphor to physics in games like Angry Birds where the physics simulation enabled exciting and surprising ways to defeat all the pigs (blocks tumbling into others, etc.).
- We co-developed Comme il Faut (CiF) along with the game Prom Week. Prom Week is a social simulation game where the player chooses what high school characters say and do with one another in the week leading up to their prom. The individual characters deires, and how characters responded to one another, was determined by CiF.
- CiF made use of over 5,000 influence rules that Ben and I wrote together capturing "social common sense" (or at least what we thought was common sense). 
- These influence rules ranged from straight forward logical assertions like "if you are my friend then I will be nice to you", but they got fairly involved like "if I have a crush on you, and someone was mean to me, and then you were mean to them, then I think 'you stood up for me! I love you even more!'". That sort of thing.
- This was largely succesful and Prom Week wa nonimated for Technical Excellence at the Independent Games Festival and was featured at several other events like Indiecade.
- Around this time, I created a game generation system that used a similar approach for refining generated games (in a generate/test/repair/test, etc. loop). The system was Game-O-Matic, and while it wasn't a social simaultion system, it used very CiF-like concepts to score the utility of various repair strategies (weighted rules evaluated over a state being accumulated, and then determining the utility of some action). The state in this case was the current assignments of parameterized components in a custom component-based game engine. This is when I really started thinking that this approach is not inherently for social modeling (though it remains a compelling use case).
- CiF was later iterated upon and enhanced by Ben and other folks at UC Santa Cruz into Ensemble. Because CiF was created along side Prom Week, it was never as general or application agnostic as we would have hoped. Ensemble introduced a schema for predicate types, a new (faster) evaluation system, and several other features. Ensemble remains compelling, but the tooling around it was never quite robust enough to create something large. But its generality was a huge step forward.
- Years later in 2016, Josh and I, inspired by [Versu]("https://versu.com"), created our own concept of social practices on top of Ensemble. Social practices are patterns of turn taking social interactions where each action is chosen by a CiF-like weighted-rule utility system choosing which path the agents moved through a dialogue tree. This was used with partners at the Education Testing Service and deployed in a study of cross-cultural learning (The Ustradian Games).
- In 2024, I started working on social practices again. Like with CiF, the Ustradian Games had poisoned the Ensemble/Social Practices system. Also, the authoring was extrememly clunky. In a herculean effort, I cleaned up the system, and created a suite of tools to make both editing, sharing, loading social state and influence rules simple (with a cloud-based spreadsheet DSL) and a robust social practice authoring tool. This system, created along with Ben, is called ESP (Ensemble with Social Practices), and [it is ready for anyone to use!]("https://github.com/mtreanor/esp").
- I should also point out that Ben and I ued ESP to create a vertical slice demo of a game called "Slice of Life". Slice of Life is a Christmas Caroll themes game about a pizzaria chef revisiting his past to regain the passion for pizza and friends. That project is sadly on hold... but you can read about it here.
- The leads to Klugh. Through this 16 year perdiod, and ever since I even understood logic and GOFAI, I have always wanted a robust, expressive, and feature rich authoring system that worked precisely the way I wanted it to. I create Klugh to overcome the challenges I faced creating and using CiF, Ensemble, Game-O-Matic (and [its failed successor Game-O-Mat]("")), and ESP (though [ESP is pretty great! Check it out!]("https://github.com/mtreanor/esp")).
