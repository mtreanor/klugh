# The History of Klugh

For me, this story starts in 2010 with the creation of Comme il Faut — a project I built alongside [Josh McCoy](https://joshmccoy.com), [Ben Samuel](http://www.ben-samuel.com), [Aaron Reed](https://aaronareed.net), and others while we were all pursuing PhDs as part of the [Expressive Intelligence Studio](https://eis.ucsc.edu) at the University of California, Santa Cruz. We were advised by [Michael Mateas](https://users.soe.ucsc.edu/~michaelm/) and [Noah Wardrip-Fruin](http://www.noahwf.com). The project itself has a rich intellectual history rooted in GOFAI (good old-fashioned AI), and I was deeply inspired by the work my advisors and the studio had already done.

The central idea behind [Comme il Faut (CiF)](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/document/6732913) was to define social norms that applied to every character in a simulation, creating what we called "social physics." The metaphor to physics is deliberate: just as the physics in a game like Angry Birds enables surprising and emergent outcomes — blocks tumbling into other blocks, pigs going down in unexpected ways — social physics was meant to enable surprising and emergent social interactions between characters.

CiF was developed alongside the game [Prom Week](https://mtreanor.com/publications/promWeek-FDG2013.pdf), a social simulation game in which the player chooses what high school characters say and do with one another in the week leading up to prom. The desires of individual characters, and how those characters responded to one another, were all determined by CiF. The system made use of over 5,000 influence rules that Ben and I wrote together, capturing what we thought of as "social common sense." These rules ranged from fairly straightforward logical assertions — "if you are my friend, I will be nice to you" — to more elaborate chains of social reasoning, like: "if I have a crush on you, and someone was mean to me, and you then were mean to that person, I think 'you stood up for me — I love you even more.'" That sort of thing. Prom Week was nominated for Technical Excellence at the Independent Games Festival and was featured at several other events, including IndieCade, which was a real validation of the approach.

Around this same time, I created a game generation system called [Game-O-Matic](https://mtreanor.com/publications/Game-O-Matic-PCG2012.pdf) that used a similar approach to refine generated games through a generate/test/repair/test loop. Game-O-Matic wasn't a social simulation system, but it used CiF-like concepts to score the utility of various repair strategies: weighted rules evaluated over an accumulated state, determining which action to take next. In this case, the state was the current configuration of parameterized components in a custom component-based game engine. This is when I really started to believe that the approach wasn't inherently about social modeling — social simulation remained a compelling use case, but the underlying mechanism was far more general than that.

CiF was later iterated upon and significantly enhanced by Ben and others at UC Santa Cruz into a system called Ensemble. Because CiF had been created alongside Prom Week, it was never as general or application-agnostic as we had hoped. Ensemble addressed this by introducing a schema for predicate types, a new and faster evaluation system, and several other improvements. It was a compelling step forward in terms of generality, though the tooling around it was never quite robust enough to build something at scale (that I've seen at least, I could be wrong!).

Years later, in 2016, Josh and I — inspired by [Versu](https://versu.com) — created a concept of social practices on top of Ensemble. [Social practices](https://mtreanor.com/publications/socialframework.pdf) are patterns of turn-taking social interaction in which each action is chosen by a CiF-like weighted-rule utility system navigating agents through a dialogue tree. We deployed this system with partners at the Educational Testing Service in a study of cross-cultural learning called The Ustradian Games.

In 2024, I started working on social practices again. As had happened with CiF before it, the Ustradian Games had accumulated enough application-specific cruft that the Ensemble/Social Practices codebase had become genuinely difficult to work with. The authoring experience was also extremely clunky. In what I can only describe as a herculean effort, I cleaned up the whole system and built a suite of tools to make it usable: a cloud-based spreadsheet DSL for editing and sharing social state and influence rules, and a robust social practice authoring tool. This system, built together with Ben, is called ESP (Ensemble with Social Practices), and [it is ready for anyone to use](https://github.com/mtreanor/esp).

Ben and I also used ESP to create a vertical slice demo of a game called [Slice of Life](https://mtreanor.com/publications/fdg2025-sliceOfLife.pdf) — a Christmas Carol-themed game about a pizzeria chef revisiting his past to rediscover his passion for pizza and for the people around him. That project is sadly on hold, but you can read about it here.

Which brings us to Klugh. Across this 16-year period — and really ever since I first understood logic and GOFAI — I have wanted a robust, expressive, and feature-rich authoring system that worked precisely the way I needed it to. Klugh is my attempt to overcome every challenge I faced while creating and using CiF, Ensemble, Game-O-Matic (and [its less successful successor Game-O-Mat](https://mtreanor.com/publications/treanor_gameomat_engine.pdf)), and ESP (though [ESP is pretty great — check it out!](https://github.com/mtreanor/esp)).

You might reasonably ask: why not just use Datalog? Did you know ASP can do 100x's more than Klugh? Why did you violate such-and-such property that would guarantee whatever? The answer is that I jut don't work well unless I do things from scratch. So if I have meaninglessly reinvented the wheel, that's just how its gotta be! If you want to see what an LLM thought about the features of Klugh, [check this out](design.md).

One last thing: where does the name Klugh come from? [Smooth jazz, obviously](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earl_Klugh).
